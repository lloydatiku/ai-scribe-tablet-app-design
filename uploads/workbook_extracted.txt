
AI scribe system design workbook
A 50 page architecture and interface workbook for a tab based clinical documentation system
Prepared from the uploaded research papers, workflow notes, evaluation notes, and the TEETH provenance interface design asset. The workbook uses synthetic examples only and should be reviewed by clinical safety, privacy, legal, procurement, and informatics leads before implementation.
Intended reader
How to use this workbook
Product, clinical informatics, engineering, governance, and evaluation teams
Read pages 1 to 8 to agree the design hypothesis, use pages 9 to 19 to review the tab app interface, then use pages 20 to 42 to convert the architecture into build tasks and safety gates. Pages 43 to 53 provide evaluation, rollout, and workbook canvases.
Page 1, purpose and design hypothesis
The goal is to design an AI scribe system that behaves less like a dictation shortcut and more like a governed documentation cockpit. The system listens, transcribes, drafts, explains, verifies, and writes back to the EHR only after the human record owner has resolved flagged uncertainty. This position follows the uploaded evidence, which shows that documentation burden is real, yet also that speech recognition and AI drafting remain unsafe when treated as autonomous authors. The design hypothesis is simple. If the interface makes origin, uncertainty, and review state visible at sentence level, and if the architecture blocks unsigned or unsupported text from entering the legal record, then clinicians may gain time without surrendering control over meaning.
The workbook therefore specifies a tab based app rather than a single free text editor. Each tab owns one work activity, consent, capture, transcript review, note assembly, provenance inspection, verification, EHR write back, patient correction, analytics, and administration. The tabs share a common provenance graph so that a clinician can move from a sentence in the note to the supporting utterance, audio span, model version, template source, and review decision. Some details will need local adaptation because EHR products, consent rules, and billing regimes differ across settings.

Page 2, evidence synthesis across the corpus
The uploaded papers converge on one design gap. Existing EHRs already fragment attention through excessive navigation, dispersed information, repeated data entry, and workarounds. AI scribes can reduce typing and after hours charting, but they often move labour into consent, setup, editing, replay, verification, and final EHR transfer. The ASR evidence warns that medication names, negation, laterality, numbers, accents, brief yes or no cues, and multi-speaker turns remain vulnerable. The OpenNotes paper adds a further pressure, because generated notes may be read by patients, become legal artefacts, and create disputes over what was said, inferred, copied, or invented.
The architecture must therefore manage three coupled problems. First, it must decrease documentation burden without increasing task switching. Second, it must create a verifiable chain from utterance to transcript, transcript to draft sentence, and draft sentence to signed record. Third, it must turn passive visibility into enforceable workflow rules. This means that labels, flags, replay controls, and contestation routes are not decorative interface elements. They are the operating surface of accountability, and they must be bound to audit events, role permissions, quality thresholds, and vendor issue management.

Page 3, source map and design consequences
This page translates the evidence base into direct build requirements. The EHR usability review is used to justify low navigation design, persistent context, and fewer manual transfers. The OpenNotes analysis is used to justify provenance, contestation, and a patient visible explanation layer. The ASR review is used to justify audio linked transcript inspection, medication recall checks, and cautious use of generated text in complex encounters. The fiscal credibility review is used to justify cost traceability and budget reporting. The transparency review is used to structure the governance pattern as Passive, VOICE, and TEETH. The two uploaded notes are used to specify workflow bottlenecks and socio-technical failure pathways.
The workbook does not assume that one vendor model can solve the whole problem. It proposes a modular system in which model services may be swapped, whereas the provenance ledger, review rules, audit data, patient challenge workflow, and EHR integration contracts remain under health system governance. That separation prevents the interface from becoming a thin wrapper around a vendor black box. It also allows procurement teams to test each component against acceptance criteria before scale up.

Page 4, problem statement for the clinic
The target clinic problem is not only that clinicians type too much. The deeper problem is that clinical meaning is assembled across multiple fragile surfaces. A patient speaks in a room, the ASR system guesses words and speakers, the model compresses or expands the content, the clinician edits under time pressure, the EHR requires billing and compliance structure, and the patient may later read the final note. At each handoff, meaning can be lost, exaggerated, misattributed, copied forward, or converted into a billing oriented phrase that is poorly suited to peer communication.
The proposed system should make these handoffs explicit. A sentence in the generated note must not appear as a neutral fact until its source and review state are known. The user should see whether a sentence is transcript grounded, model summarised, template or copied forward, clinician authored, or clinician verified. This provenance should be visible at the reading locus, not buried in a log. The system should also explain what action is available, verify, edit, remove, reattribute, ask the patient, send to coding review, open an incident, or block sign off.

Page 5, user roles and accountability boundaries
The product should identify roles before features. The clinician signatory is accountable for the signed note, but the system should not hide organisational and vendor contributions behind that signature. Nurses, residents, scribes, coders, privacy officers, safety officers, patients, and vendor support teams each interact with different parts of the record lifecycle. Role definitions prevent two common errors, too much access for everyone, or too little authority for people who need to correct errors before harm occurs.
A clean accountability model also protects clinicians from becoming the only shock absorber for system failure. When the system creates an error, the audit ledger should show the model version, input evidence, template source, configuration state, and human review action. When an organisation configures productivity rules that disincentivise review, those rules should be visible in evaluation metrics. When a patient disputes a note, the correction task should route to the right owner with time limits and an auditable decision.

Page 6, non-negotiable product principles
The first product principle is evidence proximity. The user should never need to leave the sentence they are reading to understand why it exists. The second principle is review before release. Unsigned drafts are working artefacts, and patient facing or EHR committed artefacts require explicit closure. The third principle is local uncertainty. A whole note confidence score is avoided because it can conceal high risk unsupported sentences inside an apparently safe document. The fourth principle is minimum transfer. Copy and paste should be treated as a temporary fallback, not the final architecture.
The fifth principle is accountability with consequence. A label without a workflow action is passive transparency. A label that lets a clinician verify, remove, contest, route, or block is a stronger design. The sixth principle is useful restraint. The system should make concise clinically meaningful notes easier than bloated notes. The seventh principle is patient readability without erasing clinical voice. Peer communication and patient comprehension can coexist, but sometimes they need separate views, section labels, or explanation layers.

Page 7, clinical encounter lifecycle model
The system lifecycle begins before the patient enters the room. It checks whether the patient is scheduled, whether consent is required, whether the room device is online, whether the EHR context is available, and whether the correct note template has been selected. During the encounter, the system captures audio, segments speakers, builds transcript turns, identifies potential high risk utterances, and maintains a running draft state. After the encounter, the system generates or updates the note, maps sentences to evidence, flags gaps, presents the verification queue, writes approved content to the EHR, and stores audit events.
This lifecycle framing prevents the app from over focusing on the impressive middle act of note generation. The uploaded workflow note shows that friction accumulates at the start and end as much as during drafting. Consent conversations, app launch, draft latency, manual transfer, formatting repair, and audio replay can remove the expected time saving. The architecture therefore treats preparation, interaction, verification, and closure as first class stages.

Page 8, documentation burden map
Documentation burden should be measured as displaced work, not only minutes saved. A scribe may reduce typing while increasing cognitive checking, consent explanation, app management, audio replay, and EHR transfer. The burden map separates production labour from curation labour. Production labour includes typing, dictating, finding old information, and formatting. Curation labour includes checking whether generated text is faithful, pruning irrelevant detail, confirming high risk content, and reconciling a draft with billing or quality fields.
The app should instrument both categories. It should capture time to draft, time to first review, time to sign, number of unresolved flags, number of audio replays, edits per sentence, copy paste transfers, EHR screens visited, and patient correction requests. These metrics should be visible to service managers, but they should not be used to pressure clinicians into faster unsafe sign off. Measurement needs governance. A metric that improves productivity while lowering review quality should be treated as a safety signal, not as success.

Page 9, tab app shell
The app should be designed as a tablet first clinical cockpit. A left rail can hold the encounter list and patient context, while top level tabs structure the documentation work. The centre pane changes by tab, and a right drawer holds the active verification, evidence, or task context. The shell should remain stable across tabs so clinicians are not repeatedly reorienting themselves. Patient identity, encounter time, note state, consent state, recording state, unresolved flag count, and EHR connection state should always be visible.
The tab model should reduce cognitive load by grouping actions around work states. Consent and capture should not compete with note editing controls. Transcript review should not be mixed with governance analytics. The verification queue should be reachable from every tab because unresolved safety issues are cross cutting. The shell should also support keyboard and touch use, because clinicians may move between desktop, tablet, room device, and mobile fallback.

Page 10, home dashboard tab
The home dashboard should not be a sales style productivity screen. It should be a safe work triage screen. Each encounter card needs patient initials or permitted identifier, time, specialty, note state, consent state, draft age, unresolved flag count, EHR write back state, and patient correction state. The highest priority card is not always the oldest note. It may be the note with a missing medication confirmation, unsupported denial, failed EHR transfer, or approaching release deadline.
A dashboard with safety sorting helps avoid another EHR problem, invisible queues scattered across inboxes, portals, and local apps. The dashboard should support filters for my unsigned drafts, team drafts, patient correction requests, failed exports, high risk sentences, and vendor defect candidates. It should also display a restraint metric, such as note length compared with local norms, because note bloat is a system cost.

Page 11, consent and capture tab
The consent and capture tab handles the recurring work that occurs before the model can assist. The interface should provide the approved consent script, patient questions, decline reasons, local policy links, recording device status, microphone selection, network status, language preference, and whether a human interpreter is present. Consent should be recorded as a structured event, not a free text afterthought. The system should make it clear whether the patient consented to ambient recording, transcription, draft generation, model improvement use, and patient portal release, because these are not identical permissions.
The capture controls should be simple enough to use at the start of a busy consultation. A clinician should see a green ready state only when the room device, patient context, consent requirement, and EHR encounter are matched. When the patient declines recording, the system should offer a non-recording workflow such as manual note support, typed template, or post-visit dictation. This prevents patient refusal from becoming workflow failure.

Page 12, live transcript tab
The transcript tab should behave like a clinical conversation record, not like a generic meeting transcript. Turns are grouped by speaker and timestamp, with role labels and audio replay controls. The system should mark low confidence spans, possible medication names, numerals, negation, laterality, allergies, diagnoses, and short affirmative or negative cues. These marks should not interrupt the visit excessively, but they should be available immediately after the encounter when the clinician verifies the note.
The transcript also needs a correction loop. If the clinician edits a transcript turn, the system should record the edit as transcript correction and distinguish it from a note edit. If a speaker attribution is wrong, reattribution should update all downstream evidence links. If audio quality is poor, the system should preserve that fact as part of the provenance record. A polished transcript without uncertainty metadata creates false confidence.

Page 13, note workspace tab
The note workspace is the clinician's main editing surface. It can support SOAP, SBAR, problem oriented notes, discharge summaries, procedure notes, mental health notes, or local templates, yet the interaction pattern remains the same. Each sentence or structured field has a provenance token in the gutter. The token is a button. Selecting it opens the evidence popover and highlights the supporting transcript turns. Unsupported or uncertain sentences show a visible review chip and enter the verification queue.
The note should support a clinical voice layer and a compliance layer. The uploaded OpenNotes analysis suggests that AI text can blur peer communication, billing compliance, and patient comprehension into one overburdened document. A good design allows local governance to specify which sections are patient visible as written, which sections need explanatory translation, and which billing or coding support fields are hidden from the patient note but still auditable.

Page 14, provenance evidence tab
The evidence tab is the investigative surface. It answers what the system knows, where it came from, and who accepted it. The core artefact is a sentence evidence card. The card displays the note sentence, provenance class, evidence turns, transcript confidence, audio span, model prompt or template version, extraction or summarisation method, and review history. A clinician should be able to move from card to transcript, card to note, or card to incident report without reopening a separate audit tool.
The TEETH interface notes propose five provenance classes. TG means transcript grounded. MS means model summarised. TP means template or copied forward. CA means clinician authored. CV means clinician verified. These classes are not simply colours. They should be conveyed through text code, label, border pattern, and plain language, so that they remain usable for accessibility and for printed review packs.

Page 15, verification queue tab
The verification queue converts uncertainty into work. The queue should group tasks by risk, not by their appearance in the note. A medication dose mismatch, unsupported absence of chest pain, allergy uncertainty, or a generated differential diagnosis should rise above a low risk formatting issue. Each queue item includes the sentence, why it was flagged, evidence status, possible actions, and the likely consequence if unresolved. The clinician should not need to hunt through the whole note to find unresolved safety items.
Verification should also be configurable by specialty and local governance. A dermatology follow up and an emergency department chest pain visit should not have identical flag thresholds. However, local configuration must be versioned and reviewable. If a service lowers thresholds to accelerate signing, that change should be visible to clinical safety leaders and evaluators.

Page 16, patient view and correction tab
The patient view is not a second full EHR. It is a controlled preview of what the patient will see and how they can challenge possible errors. The clinician can inspect whether generated language is understandable, whether sensitive content needs context, and whether a statement might appear accusatory or confusing. The system should preserve clinical accuracy, yet it can add explanations, glossary terms, and patient oriented summaries when local policy permits.
The correction workflow is the VOICE layer. A patient can flag a sentence, select a reason, attach a comment, and submit a correction request. The request becomes a task for the clinical team with status, deadline, resolution, and amendment trail. If a patient reports a clinically serious error, the system should raise the task priority and may trigger a safety review. The patient should receive a clear response, not a generic portal message.

Page 17, EHR write back tab
The EHR write back tab is where the AI draft becomes a clinical record. The tab should show a side by side preview of the app note and the destination EHR fields. Narrative text, problem list entries, medications, allergies, diagnoses, orders, follow up instructions, and billing support fields should be mapped explicitly. If a field cannot be written through an interface, the app should show the fallback method and the added risk. Copy and paste should be logged as a weaker integration path.
The write back tab is also the last sign off gate. The system checks unresolved verification tasks, missing consent, unsupported high risk sentences, failed field mappings, and patient visibility settings. Signing should create a final hash of the exported content, record the clinician identity, and store the provenance state. If the EHR allows only text insertion, the provenance ledger should remain available in the app and linked by encounter ID.

Page 18, governance analytics tab
The governance analytics tab helps leaders see whether the system is reducing burden safely. It should avoid vanity metrics such as total drafts generated. More useful measures include time to sign, unresolved flag rate, medication flag resolution time, note length drift, copy paste frequency, patient correction volume, safety incident count, vendor defect recurrence, language or accent performance gaps, and clinician reported workload. This tab is for learning and governance, not surveillance of individual clinicians without agreed policy.
The analytics layer should join transparency with TEETH. If a vendor update causes a rise in unsupported sentences or failed EHR mappings, the system should trigger a drift review. If a service shows repeated unsafe sign attempts, the issue may be training, configuration, workload, or productivity pressure. Governance dashboards must therefore support drill down into mechanisms rather than blame.

Page 19, administration and configuration tab
The administration tab is where the organisation controls templates, specialty rules, consent scripts, retention settings, model routing, patient view rules, and EHR mapping. It should not be a hidden technical console used only by vendors. Clinical governance, informatics, privacy, and coding teams need controlled access to approve changes. Every configuration change should have an owner, rationale, start date, rollback plan, and test evidence.
Configuration is a safety surface. The uploaded evaluation note describes how template rigidity, formatting changes, weak integration, and documentation targets can shape failure. Therefore prompt templates, note formats, and coding logic should be versioned like clinical decision support artefacts. A change that improves formatting but increases hallucinated denials should fail review.

Page 20, overall system architecture
The architecture should separate clinical interface, orchestration, AI services, evidence store, EHR integration, and governance ledger. The interface should never depend on a single model response as the source of truth. The source of truth for provenance is the event and evidence graph, which records how audio became transcript, how transcript became draft sentences, how humans changed or verified those sentences, and how content was exported. The AI services are replaceable components inside a governed pipeline.
This separation matters for procurement and safety. A health system may change ASR providers, LLM providers, or EHR vendors, but it should retain the same verification logic, audit data, patient correction process, and evaluation model. The architecture below uses event driven messaging so that capture, transcription, note generation, verification, write back, and analytics can fail independently without corrupting the signed record.
[Clinical tab app] -&gt; [Encounter orchestration service] -&gt; [Consent and capture service] -&gt; [Audio store] -&gt; [ASR and diarisation] -&gt; [Transcript service] -&gt; [Evidence index] -&gt; [Draft generation service] -&gt; [Provenance engine] -&gt; [Verification and sign off service] -&gt; [EHR integration service] -&gt; [Audit ledger and analytics] -&gt; [Patient correction workflow]

Page 21, data capture layer
The capture layer begins with a governed recording state. Each encounter can be not started, ready, recording, paused, stopped, failed, or discarded. The state is tied to consent, patient context, device identity, user identity, and room context. Audio should be segmented as it is captured so that later replay can be scoped to the minimum necessary span. When policy permits, raw audio can be retained for a defined period; otherwise the system may retain only transcript, confidence, and audit metadata.
Capture quality affects every later stage. The app should detect missing microphone access, poor signal, excessive background noise, overlapping speech, and device movement. It should warn the clinician without disrupting the visit unless recording is unusable. A poor capture warning should propagate into provenance, because a sentence grounded in low quality audio deserves a different review status from a sentence grounded in clear speech.

Page 22, ASR and diarisation layer
The ASR and diarisation layer converts audio into timestamped turns with speakers. It should support streaming where feasible, but the system must accept that final transcripts may arrive after the encounter. The layer should produce multiple confidence signals, including word confidence, turn confidence, speaker confidence, and flags for overlapping speech or uncertain segmentation. It should not hide uncertainty inside a single transcript text object.
The ASR review indicates that high risk language requires special attention. The architecture should therefore include targeted detectors for medication names, doses, allergies, dates, measurements, negation, laterality, and brief affirmative or negative cues. These detectors can compare ASR output against local dictionaries, prior EHR data, and contextual rules. The output is not an automated correction. It is a verification task with evidence.

Page 23, clinical NLP and entity layer
The clinical NLP layer should transform transcript turns into structured candidates, not final facts. It identifies symptoms, diagnoses, medications, allergies, tests, plans, social context, patient concerns, clinician assessment, and follow up instructions. Each candidate remains linked to source spans and speaker roles. The layer should distinguish patient reported information from clinician assessment, and it should tag uncertainty words such as possibly, maybe, denies, worsening, improved, and not taking.
This layer supports safer drafting by providing the note generator with bounded inputs. It can also validate the generator output by asking whether each sentence has evidence, whether the speaker is correct, and whether high risk entities match the transcript. The result is a machine readable evidence graph. The graph does not remove clinician judgement, but it makes the basis of judgement inspectable.

Page 24, draft generation layer
The draft generation layer produces text from transcript evidence, prior EHR context, and local templates. It should use constrained prompts or task specific generation steps rather than asking a model to write a whole note from raw transcript. A safer pipeline generates candidate sections, then sentence level claims, then evidence links, then style and conciseness passes. Each step should be logged with model version, prompt version, input IDs, and output IDs.
The generator should prefer omission over invention. If an item is not discussed, the model should either stay silent or create a verification task rather than inserting normal review of systems text. It should be discouraged from filling unknown denials, expanding complexity language, or producing billing phrases without evidence. Local templates can still be used, but copied forward content must be labelled as TP and must not masquerade as encounter specific evidence.

Page 25, provenance engine
The provenance engine is the backbone of the product. It records sentence origin, supporting evidence, uncertainty, review state, and export state. It should operate at sentence and structured field level, because clinicians and patients do not reason over whole notes when finding errors. A provenance object links a note sentence to one or more transcript turns, audio spans, template sources, prior note sources, model outputs, or human edits. The object also records what action has been taken.
The engine should make absence of evidence visible. If a sentence has no supporting turn, the interface should not silently accept it. The sentence may be clinician authored, template based, or unsupported. Each case means something different. Clinician authored text can be valid because clinicians know things not spoken aloud. Template based text can be useful, but it needs confirmation. Unsupported model text should be blocked or removed unless the clinician rewrites it with accountability.

Page 26, verification and sign off engine
The verification engine determines which items must be resolved before sign off. It should combine hard rules and local configuration. A hard rule might block unsupported high risk medication changes. A local rule might require verification of all TP sentences in paediatrics, mental health, or emergency medicine. The engine should compute a sign off readiness state that explains exactly which conditions block closure. A disabled sign button without explanation will create frustration and workarounds.
The sign off event should be more than a timestamp. It should store the final note text, provenance state, unresolved items allowed by policy, clinician identity, EHR destination, export status, and hash of the signed artefact. If a later patient correction or safety review amends the note, the system creates an amendment chain rather than overwriting history.

Page 27, EHR integration architecture
The integration architecture should treat the EHR as a partner system, not merely a destination for pasted text. It should use standards and vendor APIs where available, with a mapping layer that understands local note templates and discrete fields. The interface should show clinicians what will be written, and the integration service should record success or failure for each object. This is necessary because an AI scribe that cannot write back safely may increase closure work.
The system should support two paths. In a fully integrated path, it can launch from the EHR, receive patient and encounter context, write approved content to the note, and populate structured fields after clinician confirmation. In a constrained path, it can produce a signed review packet and approved copy text, but it must label the path as weaker and report transfer burden. The product roadmap should move organisations from constrained to integrated paths.

Page 28, audit ledger and evidence store
The audit ledger records events, while the evidence store holds supporting artefacts. They should be designed together but separated by retention and access rules. The ledger may need longer retention because it proves who did what and when. Audio may need shorter retention because it is sensitive. Transcript and provenance data may sit between these extremes depending on policy. The architecture should allow local organisations to set retention without breaking audit continuity.
The ledger is also the basis for vendor accountability. This makes a vendor issue reproducible without sending more patient data than necessary. It also helps procurement teams compare systems using real error patterns.

Page 29, patient contestation workflow
A patient correction workflow should be designed as a service pathway, not as an inbox message with no owner. The patient selects a sentence or section, states whether the problem is factual, context, tone, privacy, or missing information, and submits the request. The system assigns a severity based on content, then routes it to a clinician or team. The response can amend the note, add explanation, decline with reason, or escalate to a safety review.
The workflow operationalises VOICE. It gives patients a way to act on transparency and creates a record of institutional response. To avoid adversarial dynamics, the language should be respectful and clear. The patient is not asked to prove a technical hallucination. They are asked what seems wrong or incomplete. The clinician receives the original sentence, evidence links, and patient statement, which reduces rework and makes correction more precise.

Page 30, vendor accountability and incident management
The vendor accountability model should treat repeated documentation defects as contract and safety matters. A vendor should receive structured defect reports when permitted, and the organisation should track whether defects are resolved, recur, or worsen after model updates. Incidents should be classified by failure type, severity, evidence condition, and downstream outcome. This design prevents all errors from being absorbed as clinician proofreading failures.
Incident management needs both technical and organisational pathways. Technical incidents include ASR deletion, diarisation error, model hallucination, unsupported template insertion, EHR mapping failure, latency, crash, or lost note. Organisational incidents include unclear consent, productivity pressure, insufficient training, unsafe configuration change, or contested patient visibility. The incident review should ask how the error became possible and why it was or was not intercepted.

Page 31, data model overview
The data model should be designed around encounters, evidence, note sentences, review events, and exports. Traditional note systems often treat the note as one blob of text. A provenance first scribe treats each sentence and structured field as an object with source, status, and history. This object model supports precise UI actions, patient correction, analytics, and audit. It also prevents a later edit from breaking the evidence chain without detection.
The model should avoid storing more sensitive data than needed. Audio segments, transcript turns, and patient identifiers should be linked through encounter IDs and access rules. Analytics should use aggregated or de-identified data when possible. Where small services or rare conditions create re-identification risk, analytics should suppress or aggregate. The design must be assessed against local privacy law and clinical record policies.

Page 33, API and event contracts
The API design should make state transitions explicit. Recording starts only after an encounter context and consent state are present. Draft generation consumes transcript and context objects and produces sentence objects. Verification updates sentence state through controlled actions rather than arbitrary text replacement. EHR write back consumes a signed note version and creates export events. Analytics consumes events and does not mutate clinical artefacts.
Events should be idempotent where possible. Clinical environments are noisy, networks fail, and clinicians move across devices. The system should be able to replay events and reconstruct state. It should also keep failed events visible. A failed export, failed model run, or unresolved verification task is a clinical workflow item, not an engineering log only.

Page 34, security and privacy model
Security should be designed around the fact that ambient recording is more sensitive than conventional typing. The system should apply least privilege, role based access, encryption in transit and at rest, short lived tokens, device registration, audit of every access to audio, and separation between identifiable data and analytics where possible. Audio replay should require a clinical or governance reason and should be logged. Bulk export of transcripts should be restricted.
Privacy is not solved by consent alone. The app should allow pause and redaction, support patient decline, restrict third party details in patient facing views, and prevent unapproved secondary use. Vendor processing must be governed through contracts, data processing terms, access restrictions, retention periods, subprocessor disclosure, and breach processes. In some settings, a local deployment or private cloud path may be preferable to remote processing.

Page 35, consent model and retention rules

Page 36, role based access and delegation
Role based access should support real clinical work, including team care and supervision. A consultant may sign. A resident may draft and request supervisor approval. Page 37, note quality and safety controls
Note quality should be treated as a clinical safety and usability outcome. The system should check completeness, conciseness, relevance, attribution, factuality, patient readability, and structured field fit. A note that is longer and more polished can still be worse if it buries clinically useful information under irrelevant text. The system should avoid generating clinical decision recommendations unless the organisation has separately approved that function. If decision support is introduced later, it should be isolated from the scribe function and evaluated under a separate safety case.

Page 38, handling uncertainty and confidence
Uncertainty should be expressed where it acts. A clinician needs to know which sentence, entity, speaker attribution, or audio span is uncertain. Raw probabilities are often poorly interpreted, so the interface should use plain language, source class, confidence bands, and clear actions. For example, rather than showing 0.62, it can say speaker uncertain, evidence from two overlapping turns, replay recommended. A technical panel can retain raw values for auditors.
The system should avoid green washing uncertainty. A note should not show an overall safe score while containing an unsupported medication change. Confidence must also be calibrated by context. A routine blood pressure follow up, emergency department resuscitation, mental health assessment, interpreted visit, paediatric encounter, or noisy ward round may need different thresholds.

Page 39, automation bias countermeasures
Automation bias appears when users over trust fluent output. AI generated clinical notes can be especially persuasive because they look complete and professionally written. The interface should interrupt fluency at the right moments. Provenance tokens, evidence popovers, missing evidence states, high risk flags, and sign off blocks all slow the user down where error consequences are higher. These controls should be targeted, because constant warnings will create alert fatigue and workarounds.
The app should use interaction design to keep clinicians engaged in clinical synthesis. It can ask the clinician to confirm the assessment and plan in their own words, show changed meaning after edits, and flag when the model produces long generic text. It can also hide low value suggestions until requested. The goal is not to make clinicians suspicious of every sentence, but to make trust proportional to evidence.

Page 43, evaluation framework
Evaluation should begin before the pilot and continue after scale up. The evaluation question is not simply whether clinicians like the scribe. It is whether the system reduces burden, maintains or improves note quality, supports patient trust, prevents unsafe content from reaching the record, and creates useful governance evidence. The framework should combine technical measures, human factors measures, workflow measures, patient measures, fiscal measures, and safety outcomes.
The evaluation design can use phased rollout, matched clinics, pre and post measurement, or randomisation where feasible. Qualitative work remains important because workflow friction often appears in narratives before it appears in aggregate metrics. Evaluation should also measure harms avoided, such as unsupported high risk sentences intercepted before sign off.

Page 44, metric definitions and dashboard
A good metric definition includes numerator, denominator, threshold, owner, and interpretation rule. Without these details, dashboard numbers become ambiguous. For example, a reduced time to sign may be good if unresolved flags also fall. It may be worrying if unsafe sign attempts rise or patient correction requests increase. Metrics should therefore be read in families rather than alone.
The dashboard should also display caveats. Reddit based evidence and scoping reviews point towards likely mechanisms, but local validation is required. A metric that looks good in routine outpatient visits may not generalise to emergency, mental health, or multilingual contexts. The dashboard should allow stratification without exposing small groups to privacy risk.

Page 45, pilot protocol
A pilot should start in a setting with manageable risk, engaged clinicians, and measurable documentation pain. Straightforward outpatient visits can test capture, draft generation, sentence provenance, and EHR export. A second phase can include more complex visits once the safety case is stronger. The pilot should not begin by forcing the tool into the highest pressure setting, because weak integration and verification burden may be magnified there.
The pilot protocol should include baseline measurement, training, consent workflow testing, silent mode testing where the app drafts but does not write back, supervised write back, patient view testing, incident review meetings, and a stop rule. A stop rule might be triggered by repeated high risk hallucinations, export mismatch, consent failure, or unacceptable clinician burden.

Page 46, implementation roadmap
Implementation should proceed through staged capability, not a single launch. The first stage is foundation, where identity, consent, capture, transcript, draft, and audit basics work. The second stage is provenance and verification, where sentence evidence links, review queue, and sign off gates become mandatory. The third stage is EHR write back, where structured integration reduces manual transfer. The fourth stage is patient correction and governance analytics. The fifth stage is optimisation, with specialty packs and vendor comparisons.
Each stage should have a measurable acceptance test and a rollback route. A system that cannot safely write back should still allow reviewed copy workflows, but it should label them as transitional. A system that cannot produce reliable transcript in a setting should not be forced into that setting.

Page 47, change management and training
Training should focus on new work patterns rather than only button clicks. Clinicians need to know when to use the tool, how to obtain consent, how to interpret provenance tokens, how to verify high risk sentences, how to avoid over trusting fluent prose, how to handle patient questions, and how to escalate defects. Training should also tell clinicians what the system will not do. Overpromising creates disappointment and unsafe shortcuts.
Change management should include peer champions, informatics support, protected time for feedback, and a rapid defect review loop. The governance team should watch for productivity pressure that discourages careful review. It should also avoid blaming individual clinicians for workarounds that signal poor integration or unrealistic configuration.

Page 48, risk register
The risk register should be maintained as a living artefact linked to incidents and evaluation metrics. Each risk has a trigger, mitigation, owner, monitoring signal, and residual risk. The register should not be limited to technical failures. It should include consent burden, patient trust, billing inflation, review fatigue, training gaps, weak EHR integration, vendor lock in, and unequal ASR performance.
The register should also state when the system should not be used. Examples may include patients who decline recording, visits with highly sensitive third party information where recording is inappropriate, environments with unusable audio, unsupported languages without interpreter workflow, or settings where clinician review time is not available. Non-use can be a safe design outcome.

Page 49, regulatory and ethics checklist
This checklist is a design aid, not legal advice. Each organisation must review local law, professional standards, patient access rules, medical device guidance where applicable, record retention rules, privacy law, information security requirements, and procurement obligations. The app should be built to document compliance decisions, because governance cannot be reconstructed after an incident if the system never recorded the basis for those decisions.
The ethics checklist should include patient autonomy, clinician workload, error correction, proportional data use, fairness, accessibility, and accountability. It should also ask whether the system increases pressure to see more patients without providing enough time for review. Efficiency that depends on rushed signing is not a safe benefit.

Page 50, product backlog and MVP scope
The MVP should prove the backbone, not every specialty feature. It should include encounter launch, consent capture, audio recording, transcript turns, draft note generation, sentence provenance classes, evidence popover, verification queue, sign off gate, manual export with audit, and basic analytics. It should not initially automate orders, diagnoses, billing, or clinical decision recommendations. These functions create higher risk and need separate evaluation.
The backlog should be ordered by safety dependency. A beautiful patient portal is not useful if the system cannot trace note sentences to evidence. Likewise, EHR write back should follow export preview and read back tests. Early technical debt in provenance is expensive because every later feature depends on sentence IDs, evidence links, and review events.

Page 51, workbook templates and canvases
This page gives the implementation team reusable prompts. Each canvas should be completed by clinical, product, engineering, and governance representatives together. Page 52, data dictionary appendix
A shared vocabulary reduces confusion across disciplines. The terms below should be adapted into product tickets, training, and governance documents. Each term should map to interface copy, database fields, and evaluation metrics. For example, unsupported sentence should mean the same thing in the clinician UI, the audit dashboard, and the incident process.
The vocabulary should also prevent misleading claims. The system should avoid calling a note AI verified. It can say clinician verified, transcript grounded, model summarised, or template copied. Words matter because they shape reliance and liability expectations.
Term
Working definition
AI scribe
Assistive system that captures speech and drafts documentation under human review.
Transcript grounded
A sentence directly supported by one or more transcript turns.
Model summarised
A sentence that compresses or abstracts one or more evidence spans.
Template text
Boilerplate or copied forward content not created from this encounter alone.
Clinician authored
Text written or materially rewritten by a human clinician.
Clinician verified
Machine generated text accepted unchanged after explicit human review.
Unsupported sentence
Generated text without evidence link or human authored status.
Sign off gate
Rule set that prevents finalisation until mandatory checks are resolved.
Patient correction
Patient initiated request to review or amend a note statement.

Page 53, references and limitations
The workbook synthesises the uploaded EHR usability review, OpenNotes AI note analysis, ASR reliability review, digital health fiscal credibility review, transparency review, AI scribe usability review, workflow note, socio-technical evaluation note, and TEETH provenance interface design asset. The design is intentionally conservative. It treats AI drafting as useful but fallible, and it builds verification, provenance, correction, and governance into the core architecture rather than as later add ons.
Limitations should be made explicit. Several uploaded studies are scoping reviews or analyses of clinician discourse, including Reddit based data, so they explain patterns and risks but do not establish population prevalence. Local pilot evidence is still needed. The workbook also does not replace legal, medical device, cybersecurity, EHR vendor, or clinical safety review. Its purpose is to give the development team a complete architecture and decision framework from which to build and evaluate a tab based AI scribe app.
